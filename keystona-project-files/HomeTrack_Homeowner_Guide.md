## Section 1: Closing on Your Home

*These are the critical documents you'll receive at closing. Organize and store them immediately — you'll need them for taxes, insurance claims, refinancing, and eventually selling.*

### Essential Closing Documents

| Document | What It Is | Why You Need It |
|----------|-----------|----------------|
| Closing Disclosure (CD) | Final itemized breakdown of your loan terms, costs, and fees | Verify all charges are correct; needed for tax deductions |
| Deed / Grant Deed | Legal document transferring property ownership to you | Proves you own the home; recorded with your county |
| Title Insurance Policy | Protection against future claims on the property's title | Protects your ownership from liens, disputes, or fraud |
| Purchase Contract | The signed agreement between you and the seller | Reference for contingencies, agreed repairs, inclusions |
| Promissory Note | Your promise to repay the mortgage loan | Outlines your loan amount, interest rate, and repayment terms |
| Deed of Trust / Mortgage | Secures the lender's interest in your property | Records the lien; needed to release when paid off |
| Survey / Plat Map | Official property boundary documentation | Confirms your lot lines, easements, and property dimensions |
| Home Inspection Report | Inspector's findings on the home's condition | Baseline condition record; reference for future repairs |
| Appraisal Report | Lender's assessment of fair market value | Establishes baseline value; useful for tax disputes |
| Homeowner's Insurance Policy | Your dwelling and liability insurance declaration | Required by lender; needed for any future claims |
| Property Tax Records | Current assessed value and tax obligations | Understand your annual tax burden; needed for deductions |
| HOA Documents (if applicable) | CC&Rs, bylaws, rules, and fee schedules | Know your obligations, restrictions, and community rules |
| Lead Paint Disclosure | Disclosure of known lead-based paint (pre-1978 homes) | Health safety; seller legal obligation |
| Seller's Disclosure Statement | Seller's known issues with the property | Legal record of what was disclosed to you |
| Termite / Pest Inspection | Wood-destroying organism inspection report | Identifies existing or potential pest damage |

> **HomeTrack Tip:** Upload all closing documents to your Document Vault immediately. Set a calendar reminder for 30 days post-close to review everything and confirm all documents have been recorded with the county.

### Document Retention Guidelines

*Not all documents need to be kept forever, but some absolutely do. Here's how long to retain each category.*

| Document Category | Recommended Retention Period |
|------------------|---------------------------|
| Ownership Documents (Deed, Title) | Entire ownership + 7 years after sale |
| Insurance Policies | Current policy + 3 years of prior policies |
| Warranties | Until expiration + 1 year |
| Maintenance Records | Entire ownership (valuable for resale) |
| Permits & Inspections | Permanent (transfers with property) |
| Financial / Tax Documents | 7 years minimum (IRS guideline) |
| Appliance Manuals | Until appliance is replaced |
| Contractor Agreements | Duration of warranty + 2 years |

## Section 2: After You Close — First 30 Days

*Congratulations on your new home! The first 30 days are critical for getting organized. Use these checklists to set yourself up for success.*

### Immediate Actions (Days 1–7)

- [ ] Change all exterior door locks and garage codes
- [ ] Locate and label water main shutoff valve (photograph it)
- [ ] Locate and label gas shutoff valve (photograph it)
- [ ] Locate and label electrical panel / main breaker (photograph it)
- [ ] Test all smoke detectors and carbon monoxide detectors
- [ ] Replace batteries in all smoke / CO detectors
- [ ] Locate and test fire extinguishers (buy if not present)
- [ ] Set up mail forwarding with USPS
- [ ] Update address with bank, employer, insurance, DMV, voter registration
- [ ] Transfer or set up utilities (electric, gas, water, sewer, trash)
- [ ] Set up internet and cable / streaming services
- [ ] Introduce yourself to neighbors
- [ ] Walk the full property — document any damage or concerns with photos

### First 30 Days Setup

- [ ] File homestead exemption (if applicable in your state)
- [ ] Schedule HVAC inspection and filter replacement
- [ ] Have the dryer vent professionally cleaned
- [ ] Flush the water heater to remove sediment
- [ ] Clean or replace range hood filter
- [ ] Deep clean before unpacking — easier with empty rooms
- [ ] Test all GFCI outlets (kitchen, bathrooms, garage, exterior)
- [ ] Locate and review your home's circuit breaker directory
- [ ] Check attic for insulation levels and any visible damage
- [ ] Inspect crawlspace (if applicable) for moisture or pests
- [ ] Check all windows and doors for proper sealing
- [ ] Research and schedule a termite bond / pest control plan
- [ ] Set up a home maintenance binder or digital system (like HomeTrack!)
- [ ] Register all new appliance warranties with manufacturers
- [ ] Create a home inventory for insurance purposes with photos
- [ ] Review your homeowner's insurance policy for adequate coverage
- [ ] Confirm your property tax escrow is set up correctly

> **Emergency Preparedness Tip:** Set up your Emergency Hub right away: photograph all utility shutoff locations, save emergency contractor numbers (plumber, electrician, HVAC), and note your insurance policy number and claims phone number. When disaster strikes, you won't have time to search.

## Section 3: Warranties — What to Track & When They Expire

*Warranties are one of the most commonly lost and forgotten documents. A missed warranty claim can cost you hundreds or thousands of dollars. Track every warranty from day one.*

### Types of Warranties

| Warranty Type | Typical Coverage | Typical Duration |
|--------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Builder's Warranty (New Construction) | Structural defects, workmanship, materials, systems | 1 year workmanship, 2 years systems, 10 years structural |
| Home Warranty (Service Contract) | Major systems and appliances repair/replacement | 1 year (renewable annually) |
| Manufacturer's Warranty | Defects in materials and workmanship for specific products | Varies: 1–10+ years depending on product |
| Contractor / Workmanship Warranty | Labor and installation quality for completed work | 1–5 years depending on contractor |
| Extended Warranty (Purchased) | Extends manufacturer coverage beyond original term | Varies: 1–5 additional years |
| Roof Warranty | Materials and/or installation | Materials: 20–50 years; Labor: 5–10 years |

### Warranty Tracking Checklist

*For every warranty in your home, capture the following information:*

- [ ] Product / system name
- [ ] Brand and model number
- [ ] Serial number
- [ ] Purchase / installation date
- [ ] Warranty expiration date
- [ ] Coverage details (what's included)
- [ ] Retailer / installer name
- [ ] Claim phone number or process
- [ ] Receipt or proof of purchase
- [ ] Copy of warranty document

### Common Home Warranties to Track

| Item | What to Record | Expiration Reminder |
|------|---------------|-------------------|
| HVAC System | Brand, model, installer, installation date, warranty terms | Set reminder 90 days before expiration |
| Water Heater | Brand, model, serial number, installation date | Track both parts and labor warranty separately |
| Roof | Material type, installer, installation date, material vs labor warranty | Material warranties often outlast labor warranties |
| Appliances (each) | Brand, model, serial, purchase date, retailer | Register with manufacturer within 30 days of purchase |
| Garage Door Opener | Brand, model, installation date | Typically 5–10 year warranty |
| Windows & Doors | Manufacturer, installation date, transferability | Some have lifetime warranties — confirm if transferable |
| Plumbing Fixtures | Brand, model, finish, purchase date | Many have limited lifetime warranties on finish |
| Electrical Panel | Manufacturer, installation date, electrician | Typically 10–25 year warranty |
| Paint (Exterior) | Brand, color codes, application date | Workmanship warranty from painter: 2–5 years |
| Flooring | Material, brand, installer, installation date | Varies widely: 5 years to lifetime |
| Siding | Material, brand, installer, installation date | Vinyl: lifetime; Fiber cement: 15–30 years |
| Foundation / Waterproofing | Company, method, installation date | Often 10–25 year transferable warranties |

> **Expiration Alert Strategy:** Set three reminders for each warranty: 90 days before (time to plan), 30 days before (time to act), and 7 days before (final chance). HomeTrack's Document Vault does this automatically.

## Section 5: Repairs & Ongoing Maintenance

*Proactive maintenance prevents costly emergency repairs. This section covers what to do, how often, and what to document when repairs happen.*

### Seasonal Maintenance Schedule

#### Spring

- [ ] Schedule annual AC service / tune-up
- [ ] Clean gutters and downspouts
- [ ] Inspect roof for winter damage (missing/cracked shingles)
- [ ] Check exterior caulking and weather stripping
- [ ] Service sprinkler system and check for leaks
- [ ] Power wash siding, deck, and driveway
- [ ] Inspect and repair window screens
- [ ] Check grading around foundation (should slope away from house)
- [ ] Trim trees and bushes away from the house
- [ ] Test sump pump (pour water to confirm it activates)

#### Summer

- [ ] Check and clean dryer vent and ductwork
- [ ] Inspect and clean deck / patio (seal wood decks annually)
- [ ] Check exterior paint for peeling or cracking
- [ ] Inspect and test garage door safety sensors
- [ ] Clean refrigerator coils
- [ ] Check toilet components for leaks or running
- [ ] Inspect hose bibs and outdoor plumbing for leaks
- [ ] Deep clean garbage disposal
- [ ] Check attic ventilation and insulation

#### Fall

- [ ] Schedule annual furnace / heating service
- [ ] Clean gutters and downspouts (again after leaves fall)
- [ ] Winterize sprinkler system (blow out lines)
- [ ] Seal gaps around doors and windows
- [ ] Have chimney inspected and cleaned (if applicable)
- [ ] Test heating system before cold weather arrives
- [ ] Reverse ceiling fans to clockwise (push warm air down)
- [ ] Drain and store garden hoses; shut off exterior faucets
- [ ] Check weather stripping on all exterior doors
- [ ] Stock up on furnace filters for winter
- [ ] Test smoke and CO detectors; replace batteries

#### Winter

- [ ] Monitor for ice dams on roof
- [ ] Keep walkways and driveways cleared of ice
- [ ] Check pipe insulation in unheated areas
- [ ] Know how to shut off water main in case of burst pipe
- [ ] Change HVAC filter monthly during heavy use
- [ ] Check for drafts around windows and doors
- [ ] Test garage door opener safety reverse
- [ ] Clean range hood filter
- [ ] Inspect and clean bathroom exhaust fans
- [ ] Review home insurance policy for adequate coverage

### Monthly Recurring Tasks

- [ ] Replace/check HVAC air filter
- [ ] Test smoke and CO detectors
- [ ] Run water in unused drains (prevent P-trap drying)
- [ ] Clean garbage disposal
- [ ] Check under sinks for leaks
- [ ] Inspect fire extinguisher gauge

### Documenting Repairs

*Every repair — whether DIY or professional — should be documented. This builds your home's maintenance history, which is invaluable for warranty claims, insurance, and resale.*

| Information to Capture | Why It Matters |
|-----------------------|---------------|
| Date of repair | Timeline of maintenance for resale and warranty claims |
| Description of work performed | Proves specific issues were addressed |
| Contractor name and contact info | Reference for future work or warranty claims |
| Cost (parts and labor) | Track spending; tax deductions for home office/rental |
| Receipt or invoice (photo/PDF) | Proof of payment; warranty claim support |
| Before and after photos | Visual proof of improvement; valuable for resale |
| Permit number (if required) | Proves work was done to code; critical for resale |
| Warranty on work performed | Know your coverage if the repair fails |

## Section 6: Insurance — What to Have & What to Track

*Insurance is your financial safety net. Understanding your coverage and keeping policies organized can save you thousands when you need to file a claim.*

### Insurance Policies to Maintain

| Policy Type | What It Covers | Key Details to Track |
|------------|---------------|---------------------|
| Homeowner's Insurance (HO-3) | Dwelling, personal property, liability, additional living expenses | Policy #, carrier, dwelling coverage amount, deductible, premium, renewal date |
| Flood Insurance | Flood damage (NOT covered by standard homeowner's) | Policy #, coverage amount, premium, flood zone designation |
| Earthquake Insurance | Earthquake damage (NOT covered by standard homeowner's) | Policy #, coverage amount, deductible percentage |
| Umbrella Policy | Additional liability coverage beyond homeowner's limits | Policy #, coverage amount, underlying policy requirements |
| Home Warranty | Repair/replacement of systems and appliances | Policy #, covered items, service call fee, renewal date |

## Section 7: Preparing to Sell Your Home

*When it's time to sell, having organized records can increase buyer confidence, justify your asking price, and speed up the transaction. Start preparing well before you list.*

### Documents Buyers and Agents Will Request

| Document | Why Buyers Want It | Where to Find It |
|----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Property Deed | Confirms clear ownership | County recorder's office or your closing docs |
| Survey / Plat Map | Verifies property boundaries and easements | Original closing documents |
| Title Insurance Policy | Confirms no liens or title issues | Original closing documents |
| Property Tax Records | Shows annual tax obligation | County assessor's website or tax bills |
| HOA Documents | Rules, fees, financial health of the HOA | HOA management company |
| Mortgage Payoff Statement | Amount needed to release the lien | Request from your lender |
| Home Inspection Reports | Shows condition and any known issues | Your records from purchase or recent inspections |
| Permit Records | Proves renovations were done to code | City/county building department or your records |
| Appliance Manuals & Warranties | Shows what's covered and how to operate | Your Document Vault or files |
| Maintenance Records | Demonstrates responsible care of the home | Your maintenance log |
| Utility Bills (12 months) | Shows average operating costs | Utility provider accounts |
| Insurance Claims History | Discloses past damage and repairs | Your insurance carrier (CLUE report) |
| Renovation Documentation | Before/after photos, contractor info, costs | Your records |

### Pre-Listing Preparation Checklist

- [ ] Get a pre-listing home inspection to identify issues before buyers do
- [ ] Complete any deferred maintenance (leaky faucets, cracked caulk, etc.)
- [ ] Gather all warranty documents — active warranties transfer to the buyer
- [ ] Compile a complete maintenance history showing responsible ownership
- [ ] Collect all permit records for any renovations or additions
- [ ] Order a CLUE report to know your insurance claims history
- [ ] Document all recent improvements with before/after photos
- [ ] Create a list of all upgrades and improvements with dates and costs
- [ ] Prepare a seller's disclosure statement with your agent
- [ ] Organize utility bills from the past 12 months
- [ ] Gather appliance manuals and warranty documents to leave for the buyer
- [ ] Note any transferable warranties (roof, windows, HVAC, etc.)
- [ ] Deep clean and stage the home
- [ ] Generate a Home History Report summarizing your home's care

### What Makes a Home History Report Valuable

*Think of it as a Carfax for your home. A comprehensive report that documents everything about your property's care and condition.*

| Report Section | What to Include |
|---------------|----------------|
| Property Overview | Address, year built, square footage, lot size, bedrooms/bathrooms |
| System Inventory | All major systems with ages, brands, models, and warranty status |
| Maintenance Timeline | Complete service history with dates, contractors, and costs |
| Improvement Documentation | Projects with before/after photos, permits, and costs |
| Investment Summary | Total maintenance spending, improvement costs, annual averages |
| Active Warranties | All warranties that transfer to the new owner |
| Upcoming Maintenance | Known upcoming service needs or system replacements |

> **Selling Advantage:** Homes with documented maintenance histories sell faster and often for more money. Buyers gain confidence when they can see that a home has been well-maintained. HomeTrack's Home History Report feature generates this automatically from your data.

## Section 8: Emergency Preparedness

*When an emergency strikes, you need information instantly. Set up your emergency reference before you need it.*

### Utility Shutoff Reference

| Utility | What to Document | What You'll Need |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Water Main Shutoff | Location in home, valve type (ball/gate), direction to close | Photo of location, adjustable wrench or shutoff key |
| Gas Shutoff | Meter location, house-side valve location, direction to close | Photo of meter, gas wrench (keep near meter) |
| Electrical Main Breaker | Panel location, main breaker identification, circuit directory | Photo of panel and directory, flashlight nearby |
| Individual Water Valves | Locations for toilets, sinks, washing machine, water heater | Photos of each; usually hand-turn valves |

### Emergency Contacts to Save

| Contact | Information to Record |
|---------|---------------------|
| Emergency Plumber (24/7) | Name, phone, after-hours number |
| Emergency Electrician (24/7) | Name, phone, after-hours number |
| HVAC Technician | Name, phone, service contract number |
| General Contractor | Name, phone, specialties |
| Insurance Agent | Name, phone, email, policy number |
| Insurance Claims Line | Phone number, what to have ready when calling |
| Gas Company Emergency Line | Phone number (usually printed on your bill) |
| Water Company | Phone number and account number |
| Poison Control | 1-800-222-1222 (national) |
| Non-Emergency Police | Local number (not 911) |
| Trusted Neighbor | Name, phone, spare key location |

> **Offline Access Is Critical:** During emergencies, your internet may be down. Make sure emergency info is accessible offline — either printed, saved to your phone's local storage, or in an app like HomeTrack that caches emergency data locally.
